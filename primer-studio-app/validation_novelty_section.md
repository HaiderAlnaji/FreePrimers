## 1. Overview

Primer Studio integrates five specialist design engines in one web application: tetra-primer ARMS optimization, miRNA stem-loop family discrimination, SNP-aware primer-placement scoring, bisulfite methylation assay design (MSP/BSP/MS-HRM), and high-resolution melt (HRM) genotyping design. Each engine targets a documented limitation of existing tools. Below, each engine's mechanistic novelty is stated, followed by the computational validation performed and the gaps that remain.

---

## 2. Engine-by-engine novelty and validation

### 2.1 Tetra-primer ARMS optimizer

**Novelty.** Tetra-primer ARMS-PCR (Ye et al., 2001) uses four primers whose *joint* properties set allele discrimination, band resolvability, and efficiency. Primer1 and BatchPrimer3 apply sequential per-primer heuristics and do not expose a discrimination score. Primer Studio formulates design as a multi-objective search over the four-primer system, scoring allele discrimination (empirical Taq-extension model, below), Tm balance across all four primers, GC balance, hairpin ΔG and cross-dimer ΔG (primer3-py nearest-neighbour thermodynamics, SantaLucia 1998), allele-band separation, amplicon-size compliance, and primability.

The discrimination model is the mechanistic departure. Instead of a pass/fail 3′ mismatch test, it uses the −log₁₀ relative Taq extension efficiencies for the twelve 3′-terminal mispairs (Huang et al., 1992) plus a small penultimate (−2) deliberate-mismatch contribution (Simsek & Adnan, 2000). `[VERIFY]` All extension constants in `empirical_discrimination.py` are transcribed as order-of-magnitude (log₁₀) values and must be checked against the primary papers before submission.

**Verified here (in-silico run).** On a 350 bp region with a central C/T SNP, the optimizer returned **3 ranked designs** (best composite score 1.26). The top design produced **three resolvable bands — control 239 bp, allele-2 181 bp, allele-1 101 bp** (gaps 58 bp and 80 bp, both above the 40 bp minimum). The four primers were Tm-balanced to within **0.2 °C** (FO 67.9 °C/46 % GC; FI 67.8 °C/54 % GC, detecting T with a −2 C mismatch; RI 68.0 °C/65 % GC, detecting C with a −2 C mismatch; RO 67.8 °C/58 % GC). The wrong-allele 3′ suppression saturated the model cap for both inner primers (FI and RI = 5.0 log). This confirms the optimizer (i) returns multiple ranked four-primer solutions, (ii) places asymmetric outer primers to give three separable bands, and (iii) applies the terminal + −2 discrimination logic to each inner primer.

**Prior author benchmark (to re-run, not reproduced here).** Your earlier ARMS-vs-Primer1 comparison (the optimizer winning a majority of metrics across your SNP panel) cannot be reproduced in this build: the benchmark panel (`fixed_panel.py`) and the Primer1-designed competitor sets are not in this code drop. Re-run that comparison with the panel and competitor primers in hand, and report the per-SNP, per-metric outcome. Do **not** state a "3/3 SNPs / ≥4/6 metrics" result in the manuscript until it is regenerated, because that specific figure was never produced by an executed benchmark here.

**Gaps.** No wet-lab allele-specific amplification yet. The discrimination score is a ranking model, not a predicted ΔCq.

### 2.2 miRNA stem-loop RT-qPCR discriminator

**Novelty.** Stem-loop RT-qPCR of mature miRNAs (Chen et al., 2005; Varkonyi-Gasic & Hellens, 2011) needs a forward primer that resolves the target from family members. The geometric constraint other tools ignore: a discriminating base helps the forward-primer's *kinetic* blockade only if it lies **outside** the 3′ 6-nt RT-annealing window (so the forward primer's 3′ end can reach it). A difference **inside** the RT window can only be exploited at the (weaker) RT step. Primer Studio classifies each sibling's difference into *forward-terminal* (strong), *RT-window* (weak, capped at 1.0 log), *internal*, or *identical*, and labels STRONG vs weak per sibling.

**Verified here (real miRBase let-7 sequences).** Target hsa-let-7a; the forward primer's 3′ end sits at position 15 (RT window = the last 6 nt, positions 17–22):

| sibling | difference position | regime | discrimination (log) |
|---|---|---|---|
| hsa-let-7b | 3 nt from fwd-3′ (inside RT window) | RT-window | 1.0 (weak) |
| hsa-let-7c | 3 nt from fwd-3′ (inside RT window) | RT-window | 1.0 (weak) |
| hsa-let-7f | 10 nt from fwd-3′ (reaches fwd terminus) | forward-terminal | 3.5 (strong) |

This recapitulates a known experimental reality: **let-7a vs let-7b/c differ near the 3′ end (inside the RT anneal), so a forward primer alone discriminates them only weakly** — the documented reason let-7a/b/c are hard to separate without LNA/modified primers — **whereas let-7a vs let-7f differs at a mid-body position the forward primer reaches, giving strong discrimination.** (This corrects an earlier draft that stated let-7a/b/c differ *outside* the RT window; the executed engine shows the opposite, and the engine is correct.)

**Gaps.** Cross-reactivity needs wet-lab RT-qPCR with synthetic templates; the score is a ranking, not calibrated to ΔCq.

### 2.3 SNP-aware primer-placement scorer

**Novelty.** A primer over a common variant causes allele dropout or Cq bias in the bound allele; Primer3 does not model this. Primer Studio computes a continuous positional risk: the 3′-terminal base carries maximum weight, dropping steeply over positions 1–5 from the terminus and shallowing internally, scaled by minor-allele frequency. `[VERIFY]` the position-weight curve is built from published qualitative mismatch-effect descriptions (terminal mismatches → large Cq shift; internal → allele bias), not a regression on efficiency data — cite the supporting sources precisely or soften the claim.

The algorithmic distinction is **max-dominated (non-additive) aggregation**: one high-MAF 3′-proximal variant should dominate the total regardless of how many low-risk variants are present.

**Verified here (in-silico).** Scoring a 20-nt forward window:
- one terminal variant at MAF 0.30 → **total risk 0.850, verdict "avoid"**;
- five internal variants at MAF 0.05 → **total risk 0.560, verdict "caution"**.

The single terminal high-MAF SNP **outranks five internal low-MAF SNPs**, which a naive additive score would invert (it would rank the five-variant window as worse). This is the intended position × frequency behaviour.

**Gaps.** The score is a risk ranking, not a quantitative ΔCq/efficiency predictor; prospective validation against curated primer–SNP outcomes is needed.

### 2.4 Methylation assay designer (MSP / BSP / MS-HRM)

**Novelty.** One engine takes a single unconverted genomic input and internally builds both converted strands — unmethylated (all C→T) and methylated (CpG cytosines retained, non-CpG C→T) — then designs MSP (CpG-discriminating), BSP (non-discriminating, CpG-flanking), or MS-HRM (short amplicon whose melt reflects methylation density). CpG-island context uses Gardiner-Garden & Frommer (1987): ≥200 bp, GC% ≥ 50, observed/expected CpG ≥ 0.6.

**Verified here (assertion-checked).** Bisulfite conversion on a test sequence carrying both CpG and non-CpG cytosines passed both invariants: **(1)** every non-CpG C converts to T on both strands — PASS; **(2)** CpG cytosines are retained on the methylated strand and converted on the unmethylated strand — PASS. CpG-island scanning of a 320 bp synthetic sequence (AT-rich flanks around a GC/CpG-dense core) flagged a single island interval spanning the core (positions 41–279) and excluded the AT flanks. *Audit note:* the observed/expected ratio can inflate in windows with very few C or G (boundary windows); because the island call also requires GC% > 50, these do not produce false-positive island calls, but capping o/e or requiring minimum C and G counts would make the track more robust.

**Prior claim removed.** The earlier draft's "CpG-island concordance with the UCSC track for MGMT/MLH1/BRCA1 promoters" was not produced by any executed check and has been removed; if you want that comparison, fetch the UCSC CpG-island track for those loci and score concordance explicitly.

**Gaps.** No wet-lab MSP/BSP PCR. Incomplete bisulfite conversion and CpH methylation are not modelled. MS-HRM melt curves are predicted (see §2.5 model note), not instrument-calibrated.

### 2.5 HRM genotyping designer (standard + allele-specific) — corrected Tm model

**Novelty / what changed and why.** HRM discriminates genotypes by amplicon melt. The amplicon Tm model went through three stages, and the reason for the final change is itself a result worth reporting:

1. *Bulk Wallace/Wetmur %GC formula* — overestimated amplicon Tm during earlier development (your prior observation).
2. *Two-state nearest-neighbour (SantaLucia 1998) on the whole amplicon* — fixed the overestimate **but introduced a clustering artefact**: it returned ~79–80 °C for many different SNP amplicons.
3. *Empirical product (amplicon) Tm formula* — the current model.

**Verified here — root cause of the clustering.** Applying the two-state NN equation to a whole 60–120 bp amplicon is the wrong physical regime. As the duplex lengthens, the strand-concentration term `R·ln(C_T/4)` becomes negligible against the length-scaled entropy, so Tm collapses to the ΔH/ΔS ratio — a function of **GC composition only**, with the length term effectively gone. Executed evidence:
- at fixed 50 % GC, increasing length 80 → 1000 bp moved NN Tm only **80.1 → 86.9 °C**, whereas 20 → 40 bp moved it **58.5 → 72.8 °C** — length sensitivity is already lost in the HRM size range;
- the GC slope is only **~2.4 °C per 5 % GC**.

Human SNP flanks are GC-homogeneous (mostly 45–55 % GC), so every ~90 bp amplicon lands in a narrow band → **the observed 79–80 °C for "different" sequences is a model artefact, not melting behaviour.** The earlier draft's claim that the NN model "correctly predicts the 75–85 °C range" was therefore misleading in this regime; the NN model was the *cause* of the clustering, not the fix.

**The fix.** The current model is the standard empirical product-Tm equation, with monovalent-salt correction:

```
Tm = 81.5 + 0.41·(%GC) − 600/L + 16.6·log10([Na+])
```

It keeps a real, monotonic length term (`−600/L` does not asymptote like two-state NN) and a GC term. Executed results:
- **De-clustering:** across amplicons of 40–62 % GC the predicted homozygote Tm now spans **~7.6 °C** (vs ~3 °C clustered before).
- **HRM-class behaviour reproduced** (single-base homozygote ΔTm shifts only when the SNP changes amplicon GC count):

| HRM class | substitution | homozygote ΔTm (verified) | call |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | C/T | 0.46 °C | resolvable by amplicon HRM |
| 2 | C/A | 0.46 °C | resolvable |
| 3 | C/G | 0.000 °C | near-zero → AS-HRM recommended |
| 4 | A/T | 0.000 °C | near-zero → AS-HRM recommended |

This is exactly the documented difficulty pattern (Class 3–4 give ~0 amplicon-Tm shift). The engine reports the class via `snp_hrm_class()` and warns for Class 3–4 in standard mode.

**Honest limitations of the closed form (stated for the manuscript).** The product-Tm formula depends only on length, %GC and salt, so (i) two amplicons of identical length and GC return the same Tm even if their internal sequence differs, (ii) it cannot finely separate Class 1 from Class 2 (both give ΔTm ≈ 0.41·100/L), and (iii) amplicons of *similar* length/GC genuinely melt at *similar* Tm — that part of the clustering is real physics, not a bug. **For HRM genotyping the operative signals are curve shape, the heteroduplex (lower-melting) trace, and the genotype Tm *difference*, not the absolute amplicon Tm.** A Poland-Scheraga statistical-mechanics melt (uMELT/uAnalyze; Dwight, Palais & Wittwer, 2011) is the accurate reference and is the principled future upgrade for true sequence-arrangement sensitivity. All Tm values are labelled *predicted*.

**AS-HRM and MS-HRM figures (fixed).** Both modes now emit the same figure set as standard HRM:
- *Standard HRM* — three genotype melt curves (a1/a1, a2/a2, a1/a2) + difference (−dF/dT) plot.
- *AS-HRM* — the expected allele-specific **product** melt with its own difference trace, plus an explicit note that AS-HRM genotypes by *which allele amplifies* (ARMS-style 3′ discrimination), the melt only confirming a single clean product; this replaces the earlier reuse of the standard genotype-melt model, which produced near-overlapping, uninformative curves for AS-HRM.
- *MS-HRM* — methylated vs unmethylated amplicon melt + difference plot (previously the methylation panel rendered no melt figure and the backend curve lacked the difference trace; both fixed). On a CpG-island test amplicon the predicted methylated–unmethylated Tm gap was **~7.9 °C** (unmethylated 68.1 °C, methylated 76.0 °C), the MS-HRM discrimination signal.

**Gaps.** Predicted curves use a sigmoidal melting model anchored at the product Tm; they are qualitative, not instrument-calibrated (dye, scan rate, format). No prospective genotyping accuracy yet.

## 4. Comparison with existing tools

Primer3 (Untergasser et al., 2012) is the generic reference but implements no allele-specific discrimination, bisulfite-context design, or SNP-overlap risk scoring. Primer1 (Marshall et al., 2010) designs tetra-primer ARMS sets via sequential heuristics and exposes no discrimination score. BatchPrimer3 (You et al., 2008) adds batching but inherits Primer3's lack of assay-specific discrimination. MethPrimer (Li & Dahiya, 2002) designs MSP/BSP but handles one assay type per session and lacks MS-HRM. To our knowledge no public tool implements the regime-aware miRNA stem-loop discriminator or the max-dominated SNP-placement scorer described here. `[VERIFY]` confirm current feature sets of each competitor at time of submission (tools are updated).

Primer Studio exposes every engine via a documented REST API (FastAPI, auto-generated OpenAPI) and a browser-deployable React frontend.

## 5. Limitations

All validation here is computational, using executed engine outputs and published models as ground truth. Prospective wet-lab validation — synthesis, PCR, electrophoresis, sequencing, HRM instrument runs — is the necessary next step and is deferred. The empirical discrimination values (ARMS, miRNA) derive from Taq kinetics and are not directly transferable to high-fidelity/hot-start enzymes without re-parameterization. SNP-placement risk assumes standard PCR conditions. The methylation engine does not model incomplete conversion or CpH methylation. HRM amplicon Tm is a closed-form approximation (see §2.5); arrangement-level accuracy requires a Poland-Scheraga melt.
